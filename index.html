The Problem

Chapters 1 and 2 handled simple sentences. But most real arguments are about sets — "All SACCO members must contribute," "Some Negators missed the meeting."

You need quantifiers to say "all" or "some."

∀ — Universal ("for all")

∀x (Negator(x) → Contributes(x)) — "All Negators contribute."

A single counterexample destroys it. One member stops paying → false.

∃ — Existential ("there exists")

∃x (Negator(x) ∧ Confirmed(x)) — "Some Negators confirmed."

One example makes it true. Shan and Derrick confirmed → true.

The Flip Rule

StatementNegation
¬∀x F(x)∃x ¬F(x)
¬∃x F(x)∀x ¬F(x)

"Not all confirmed" = "There exists one who didn't." Shalvin.

The Trap

∀ always pairs with →. ∃ always pairs with ∧.

Mixing them is the #1 beginner mistake.
